Exposure (Χ)  = amount of charge produced through ionisation
  • in a mass of air
  • under standard condition
  • by indirect radiation
\( \LARGE \chi = {Q \over m} \)
\( \LARGE = \Psi \left({\mu_{en} \over \rho}\right) \)
\( \LARGE \left({e \over W_{air}}\right) \)
\( = \large K_{col} \)
where
\(W_{air}\)  =  average energy from incident radiation needed to ionise 1 ion pair
Ψ = energy flux
\(\large \frac{\mu_{en}}{\rho}\) = mass energy absorption coefficient (collision)
unit = \(C~kg^{-1}\) or R
KERMA (K) Definition
KERMA K at a volumn of the target medium is the sum of KE of
charge particles ionised
by the incoming
indirect ionising radiation
.

e.g.  Air Kerma in air = energy transfer to air surrounded by air
  Air Kerma in water = energy transfer to air surrounded by water
  Water Kerma in water = energy transfer to water surrounded by water

\[ \large K_{tot} = {dE_{tr} \over {dm}} = \Psi {\left( \frac{\bar{\mu}_{tr}}{\rho} \right)} = K_{collision} + K_{radiation} \]
where
\(E_{tr}\) = energy transferred

Kradiation = loss as radiation through bremsstrahlung
= energy not depositing in the medium

Kcollision = energy absorbed per mass of medium
= \(\Large {dE_{en} \over dm} \)

= the energy absorbed by the target medium
= \( hv\Phi \large \left( \frac{\mu_{ab}}{\rho} \right) \)

= Exposure × energy required to produce an ion pair in medium
\[ K_{col, air, air} = \large{{W_{air} \over e}} \chi \]

Transfer-Absorption Relation
g = radiative fraction
  = portion of incoming energy that will loss through radiation
\[ K_{ab} = K_{tr}(1-g) \] \[ \frac{\mu_{ab}}{\rho} = \frac{\mu_{tr}}{\rho} (1-g) \]
when Z ↓ and E < 1 MeV,
  • g = 0
  • K ≃ Kcollision

$$ \begin{align} K_{air} &= \Psi \left( \frac{\mu_{tr}}{\rho} \right)_{air} \\ &= \Phi h f \left( \frac{\mu_{tr}}{\rho} \right)_{air} \end{align} $$

Bw = backscatter factor

Exposure Rate When we compare the exposure of 2 sources, time need to be considered.

For
(1) a specific
γ/X-ray emitting
radionuclide, that is a
(2) point source, with
(3) Activity A (mCi), at a
(4) Distance of d (cm)

The exposure rate = 
\[ \large \dot{\chi} = \frac{ A\Gamma }{d^{~2}} \]
where
A = Activity
Γ = Exposure Rate Constant (for both γ and X-ray)
  / Specific Gamma-ray Constant (for γ only) [1]
d = Distance from source
Unit: R h-1

If a radionuclide is enclosed in a
wall > 0.5 mm
, a correction term needs to be added: \[ \large \dot{\chi} = \frac{ A\Gamma }{d^{~2}} e^{-\mu x} \] where
μ = linear attenuation coefficient of the wall material
x = thickness of wall
Specific Gamma-ray Constant
/ Exposure Rate Constant 		 = Exposure Rate per Activity at 1 cm
  • of a specific
    γ/X-ray emitting
    radionuclide
  • point source
  • unit = \( \large \frac{R~cm^2}{mCi~h} \) or \( \large \frac{R~m^2}{Ci~h} \)
Air Kerma Rate Constant (ΓAKR)
= unique constant for each radionuclide
= Kerma at a distance per activity per hour
unit: \( \large \frac{\mu~Gy~m^2}{GBq~h} \)


From Exposure Rate Constant to Air Kerma Rate Constant
Using
exposure rate constant ΓX,
1 R = 8.76 × 10-3 Gy in dry air, and
1 mCi = 37 MBq,

rearrange ΓX to get ΓAKR
unit = \( \large \frac{\mu~Gy~m^2}{GBq~h} \)


e.g. if we have a material,
which ΓX is given in (R.m2.Ci-1.h-1),
the ΓAKR of the material will be, \[ \Gamma_{AKR} = \frac{ (0.876 \times 10^{-2})~Gy}{ 3.7 \times 10^{10}~Bq}~\Gamma_{X} \]


Reference Air Kerma Rate
\[ RAKR = \frac{ A_{apparent} \Gamma_{AKR} }{ d^2 } \]
